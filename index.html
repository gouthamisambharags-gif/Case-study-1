The twelve minutes between shifts.

A redesign of the shift log and handover tool used across three plants. What was a stack of paper notes and hallway conversation becomes a structured, searchable memory that travels with the unit.

Role
Lead Designer
Platform
Desktop, Material 3
Overview

A handover is the moment plants are most exposed. When the tool fails, the next operator inherits blind spots, and I saw enough of those to know they compound.

We turned a free-text notepad into a structured log with typed entries, tag anchors, priority, and a signature. What follows is five screens across a full shift, from opening the log to signing it over to the next crew.

The five screens below are the ones that mattered. Twelve more didn't make it into this write-up.

Screen 01 / Open the log

A living timeline of the shift.

The log opens on the current shift, entries anchored to when they happened. Prior shifts sit one collapse away.

Screen 02 / Log an event

One panel, forty seconds.

Typed entry, tag anchor, priority, attachments. A slide-in panel keeps the log visible so operators never lose their place.

Screen 03 / Prepare handover

Ten minutes before rollover, the summary writes itself.

A pre-built summary pulls in unresolved items, watch flags, and interventions. The outgoing operator edits, adds a hypothesis, and signs.

Screen 04 / Receive & acknowledge

Shift C arrives to a briefing, not a mystery.

The incoming operator sees the same summary, formatted for reading, with unresolved items called out first. Acknowledgment is a signature.

Screen 05 / Audit trail

Every handover, forever, searchable.

A read-only history of every handover with signatures, lineage of unresolved items, and full-text search. One place for compliance, incident review, and knowledge transfer.

How I worked

Listening first, then letting AI do the heavy lifting.

The brief came out of listening, not a workshop. Interviews with operators and supervisors across a refinery, a specialty-chemical site, and a paper mill. Anonymised log text to pore through. Voice-guide drafts, dozens of them, until the tool sounded the way an outgoing operator actually talks.

AI helped me get through the volume, log corpus, interview transcripts, first drafts of copy against a voice guide. I made a rule for myself and stuck to it: AI does the pattern-recognition, I do the pattern-creation. Anything AI produced was reviewed against the source before it went near the design.

Notes to self, still pinned above the desk:

  1. Watch the handover, not the tool.
  2. The paper works. Ask what it does that the software doesn't.
  3. Every AI draft gets a human name on it before it ships.
  4. If shift C would call, the design failed.
